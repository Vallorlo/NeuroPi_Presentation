Chapter 1: EEG Fundamentals and Analysis Tools

Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non-invasive technique used to record the brain's electrical activity through electrodes placed on the scalp. This chapter covers the fundamental concepts and tools for EEG analysis.

EEG systems capture the aggregated activity of thousands of neurons working together. Key characteristics include:

  • Number of Electrodes: Typically between 1-250 depending on application
  • Continuous Data: Records brain activity over time
  • Artifacts: Noise from eye blinks, muscle movements, etc.

1.1 Introduction to Electroencephalography

Key Features of EEG

EEG provides a unique window into brain activity with several important characteristics:

Challenges in Understanding EEG

  • Source Localization: The inverse problem - determining exact origin of brain activity
  • Positive/Negative Potentials: Don't directly indicate activation/deactivation
  • Dipole Orientation: Affects signal strength and direction

Strengths of EEG

  • High Temporal Resolution: Captures activity in milliseconds
  • Cognitive Function Links: Patterns tied to specific mental states
  • Time-Series & Spatial Data: Shows how and where brain activity evolves

1.2 Time and Frequency Domains in EEG Data Analysis

Time Domain Analysis

Plots signal with time on x-axis and voltage on y-axis. Ideal for analyzing event-related responses.

Frequency Domain Analysis

Uses FFT to transform data, showing frequency on x-axis and power on y-axis. Best for periodic signals.

Time-Frequency Relationship

There's an inverse relationship between time and frequency resolution: "The more precise you are in time, the less precise you are in frequency - and vice versa."

Major EEG Frequency Bands

Band Frequency Range (Hz) Characteristics
Delta 1-3 Deep sleep, early development
Theta 4-7 Drowsiness, memory, meditation
Alpha 8-14 Relaxation (especially eyes closed)

Beta 15-30 Active thinking, focus, alertness
Gamma 30+ Higher cognitive functions, perception

1.3 Event-Related Potentials (ERP) Analysis

Definition and Concept

ERPs represent short segments of EEG data time-locked to specific events:

  • Presentation of a stimulus (image, sound, touch)
  • Onset of a motor response (pressing a button)

Core Methodology

  1. Record EEG continuously throughout experiment
  2. Segment data into short time windows (epochs)
  3. Average across many trials to reduce noise

1.4 ERP Components

Definition and Characteristics

Distinct peaks or troughs in ERP waveform characterized by:

  • Timing: When component appears after stimulus
  • Scalp Distribution: Electrodes with highest amplitude
  • Polarity: Negative (N170) or positive (P3)

1.4.2 Examples of Common Components

Component Timing Location Polarity Function
N170 170–200 ms Inferior temporal-parietal lobe Negative Visual processing of faces and words
N1 100 ms Middle frontal region Negative Auditory processing and attention
P3/P300 300 ms Posterior midline Positive Processing of task-relevant stimuli

N1 Component in Auditory Attention

Sounds that receive attention produce a stronger N1 response. Used to study how attention is spatially directed.

Experimental Design Considerations

  • Clear hypotheses about component effects
  • Focused analysis of amplitude/latency
  • Cognitive interpretation of changes
